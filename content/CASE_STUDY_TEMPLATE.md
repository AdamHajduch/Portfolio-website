## PAGE STRUCTURE

### 1. PROJECT TAGLINE
One sentence. Describes what the product/company is + what your specific contribution was.
Write it from your POV, not as a press release.

> Pattern: "[Product] is/was [what it does]. I [your specific contribution]."
> Example: "CUJO provides the power of enterprise security solutions in a simple and elegant device. I helped CUJO shape their brand and user experience."

---

### 2. METADATA BLOCK (sidebar / two-column)

**My role** — 3 to 4 bullet points. Mix of methods and deliverables. Not job titles.
- User research, prototyping, UI design
- Built and maintained a design system
- [Method or deliverable]
- [Method or deliverable]

**Results** — 3 bullet points. Outcomes, not tasks. Specific where possible, directional where not.
- [Metric or outcome #1]
- [Metric or outcome #2]
- [Metric or outcome #3]

---

### 3. HERO IMAGE
Full-width. The strongest visual — usually the finished product or a key screen.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE BODY
Repeat the following unit for each major section of the project story.
Sections flow chronologically through your process: problem → research → solution → detail → outcome.

#### NARRATIVE UNIT (repeat as needed):

**[PULL QUOTE]**
A single bold sentence. This is the insight, tension, or observation that frames this part of the story.
It should work as a standalone statement — something a reader could pause on.
Write it from the user's or your perspective. Not marketing copy.

> Example: "For an ordinary person, anything related to cyber security seems abstract and cryptic."
> Example: "In 99% of our user interviews, in order to find showtimes, people would search for a film or a cinema."

[Body paragraph(s)]
1–2 short paragraphs. Explain the problem, what you learned, the decision you made, and why.
Always connect to: user research / data / prototypes / constraints.
Use "we" when working with a team, "I" for solo decisions.
Keep paragraphs to 3–5 sentences max.

[IMAGE]
Full-width image. Can be: screens, flow, prototype, icon set, design system component.
No captions on images — the text above does the framing.

---

### 5. NAMED SUB-SECTIONS (optional, for complex feature areas)
Use a short heading when a feature or problem area is distinct enough to deserve its own label.
Headings are short nouns: "Design system", "Navigation", "Sorting & Filtering", "Seatmaps"
Apply the same narrative unit structure within each sub-section.

## WRITING RULES

**Voice**
- First person. "I" for individual decisions, "we" for team work.
- Direct and specific. No vague claims like "we improved the experience."
- Process-oriented. Always show the thinking, not just the outcome.

**Pull quotes**
- One per narrative unit.
- State a tension, observation, or user insight — not a solution.
- Should work as a standalone sentence. Read it out of context — does it make you curious?

**Body paragraphs**
- Ground every decision in: user research, data, a prototype, or a constraint.
- Explain what you tried, what you learned, what you decided, and why.
- Keep it concise. If a paragraph runs over 5 sentences, split it.

**Results / outcomes**
- State in the metadata block upfront.
- Do not repeat or over-explain outcomes in the body.
- If a result is directional (not a hard number), that's fine — "Increased MAUs and retention rate" is acceptable.

**What to avoid**
- Don't describe what users can see in the image — they can see it.
- Don't use marketing language: "best-in-class", "seamless", "intuitive", "delightful" (unless quoting users).
- Don't explain every design decision — pick the ones with the most interesting story behind them.

---

## SECTION ORDER REFERENCE
(Loosely chronological through the design process)

1. Context — what the product is, what the business challenge was
2. Research insight — what you learned from users / data that reframed the problem
3. Architecture / structure decision — how you organized the experience and why
4. Key feature areas — 2 to 4 specific problems you solved, each as a narrative unit
5. Design system (if applicable) — how you maintained consistency at scale
6. Experiments / edge cases (optional) — anything you tried that's worth sharing
7. Outcome — can be woven in naturally, not a separate "conclusion" section

## VISUAL RHYTHM NOTES (for Claude Code)

- Alternate: text block → full-width image → text block → image(s)
- Pull quotes render as large text, visually distinct from body copy
- Images are full-width or near full-width — no thumbnails, no grids (unless showing multiple screens together as a single image)
- No image captions
- Metadata sidebar appears once, at the top, before the hero image
- No inline links in body text except when referencing a related case study or an external article you wrote
