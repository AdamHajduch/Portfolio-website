## THE CORE STORY LOGIC

Every case study tells one story: **a designer who understands users deeply, makes decisions grounded in evidence, and knows how to explain their thinking.**

It's not a portfolio of pretty screens. It's proof of a mind at work.

The story is always told in the same direction:
**Constraint → Research insight → Decision → Rationale**

Never the reverse. Never: "We built X. Here's why it was great."
Always: "Here's what we were up against. Here's what we learned. Here's what we decided. Here's why."

---

## THE FIVE-BEAT STORY STRUCTURE

Each case study — regardless of length — moves through five beats:

### Beat 1: THE STAKE
What made this project hard? What was the real challenge underneath the brief?
Not "we needed to design an app." The actual tension.

> CUJO: "Most people simply do not care enough about their online safety to pay for an extra device — unless it's simple and delightful."
> YPlan: "Less is not always better. The thing our users loved was becoming a limitation."
> Cinema Club: "The cinema market does not allow third parties to access tickets or initiate booking on the user's behalf."

The stake is usually a human truth or a market constraint. It tells the reader: this wasn't a clean brief with an obvious answer.

### Beat 2: THE USER LENS
What did you learn from users that reframed how you saw the problem?
This is always research-grounded: interviews, observation, data.

> "We asked our users to define the most important questions that come to mind about their home network. Those were shortlisted to top 3."
> "In 99% of our user interviews, in order to find showtimes, people would search for a film or a cinema."
> "Talking to the users, it soon became apparent that they were surprisingly loyal to their favorite cinemas."

The user lens doesn't just validate — it often *redirects*. It shows something unexpected or counterintuitive that changed the direction.

### Beat 3: THE DECISION
What specific choice did you make, and why?
This is the crux. One clear decision, explained with precision.

Not "we designed a better navigation." Exactly what you chose and what you didn't choose.

> "From a series of prototypes, we landed on a tab-based app architecture, each dedicated to a specific aspect of the network."
> "We decided to start with a simple framework that would satisfy the basic needs of most, and iteratively build vertical specific features."
> "We based the default sorting on the cinema's distance to the user."

The decision always comes *after* the research. It never appears first.

### Beat 4: THE DEPTH
Where did the real design work happen? What was harder than it looked?
This is where sub-sections live. Each one is a new constraint worth exploring.

Each sub-problem follows the same mini-arc: what made it hard → what you tried → what you landed on.

### Beat 5: THE PAYOFF
What changed as a result? Stated briefly, matter-of-factly.
Not celebrated. Just reported.

This lives mostly in the metadata block (Results), not the body. The body doesn't end with a victory lap — it ends when the story of the work ends.

## HOW THE PULL QUOTE WORKS

The pull quote is the most important sentence in each section. Get this right and the rest follows.

**What it is:**
A single sentence that names the tension, constraint, or observation that makes the next section necessary. It frames the problem before the solution arrives.

**What it is not:**
- A summary of what you did
- A claim about the quality of the work
- A user testimonial
- A transition sentence

**Three types of pull quotes used:**

1. **The honest admission** — acknowledges a real constraint or uncomfortable truth
   > "For an ordinary person, anything related to cyber security seems abstract and cryptic."
   > "While important, customers are only one half of any marketplace."

2. **The research finding** — a specific, surprising thing you learned
   > "In 99% of our user interviews, people would search for a film or a cinema."
   > "While engaging with our community we were surprised to see how excited they were to report new threats."

3. **The reframe** — shows that the obvious solution wasn't the right one
   > "Our checkout funnel had to work for events ranging from West End Theatre shows bookable months in advance to spontaneous booking of a small local comedy show."

**How to write one:**
Take the core insight of a section. Strip it to one sentence. Make it stand on its own — if you read it without the surrounding paragraphs, it should make you want to know more.

Test: does it create a question in the reader's mind? If yes, it's working.

## THE PARAGRAPH RHYTHM

Each body paragraph follows a tight rhythm. Usually 3–5 sentences. Always with a forward direction.

**Sentence 1:** Situation or observation — what you knew, saw, or were given.
**Sentence 2–3:** What you did in response — research, prototyping, decision-making.
**Sentence 4:** What you arrived at — the specific answer.
**Sentence 5 (optional):** Why it mattered — the consequence or implication.

> "Contrary to most of our customers, theatre goers are more likely to plan months in advance, and be flexible with their dates, to get the best possible deal. Building on customer interviews and data, we developed a custom flow with the ability to select seats in the seat-plan of a specific venue, and quickly see the lowest price for any given show months in advance."

Notice: observation → evidence source → specific decision. Never jumps straight from problem to solution without showing the bridge.

## PROCESS LANGUAGE

The vocabulary of the writing is specific. These phrases carry the process-forward tone:

**To show iteration:**
- "From a series of prototypes, we landed on..."
- "Having gone through a series of iterations we landed on..."
- "Iterating through a series of prototypes we arrived at..."

**To show research grounding:**
- "Drawing on our conversations with..."
- "Building on customer interviews and data..."
- "Talking to users, it soon became apparent that..."
- "Looking at the data and talking to customers..."

**To show a specific insight leading to a decision:**
- "Building on [X], we could provide additional value by..."
- "Seeing an opportunity for [X], we..."
- "One outlier among the many prototypes was..."

**To introduce a constraint:**
- "The complex nature of [X] necessitated..."
- "While [X] was important, [Y] was highlighted as the biggest pain point..."
- "The most challenging part will be..."

Use these or variations of them. They create a sense of a thoughtful, iterative process without being verbose about it.

## WHAT THE WRITING NEVER DOES

These are the tells of generic portfolio copy. Avoid all of them:

- **Never describes what's visible in the image.** The image shows it. The text explains why it exists.
- **Never uses vague positive adjectives:** "seamless", "intuitive", "clean", "elegant", "delightful" (unless quoting a user).
- **Never explains every decision.** Only the ones with an interesting story behind them — a constraint, a surprise, a reframe.
- **Never starts with the solution.** Always earns the solution through the problem and the research.
- **Never celebrates outcomes in the body.** Results live in the metadata. The body is dispassionate about what worked.
- **Never uses passive voice.** "We decided", not "it was decided." "I developed", not "a design system was developed."
- **Never has a conclusion paragraph.** The story ends when the work ends. No wrapping up, no lessons learned section.

## FIRST PERSON RULES

"I" vs "we" is not random — it signals something:

- **"I"** = a decision or responsibility that was yours alone (role, ownership, initiative)
  > "I was tasked with...", "I helped CUJO shape...", "I developed a modular design system..."

- **"We"** = collaborative work with a team or with users
  > "We asked our users...", "We landed on...", "We designed a simple yet powerful UI..."

Don't use "the team" or "the designers." Use I or we directly.

## HOW TO BUILD A SECTION FROM RAW MATERIAL

When you have project information (description, screenshots, notes), use this process to turn it into a case study section:

1. **Identify the constraint or problem** — what made this part hard?
2. **Find the user insight** — what did you learn that changed your thinking?
3. **Name the decision** — one clear choice, explained with precision.
4. **Write the pull quote first** — the single sentence that frames the tension.
5. **Write the body paragraph(s)** — Situation → Research → Decision → Rationale.
6. **Choose the image** — the one that shows the decision, not the prettiest screen.

If you can't identify a real constraint or insight for a section, that section probably shouldn't exist.

---

## TONE CALIBRATION

The writing voice is: **confident, precise, and unsentimental.**

- Confident: no hedging ("we tried to", "we hoped to"). State what you did and why.
- Precise: specific details over general claims. "70 screens" not "many screens." "Tab-based navigation" not "better navigation."
- Unsentimental: no enthusiasm about your own work. Let the decisions speak.

The one exception is when something genuinely surprised you or went against expectations — that gets a moment of honesty:
> "While engaging with our community we were surprised to see how excited they were to report new threats."
> "One of the most exciting things about building a new product is being able to experiment and take risks without too much to lose."

These moments of honest reaction make the writing human without making it self-promotional.
